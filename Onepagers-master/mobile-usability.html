Mobile usability: thumb zones and touch targets

The thumb zone reality

Based on extensive research, here's where people can actually reach comfortably with their thumbs.

Hard to reach

Green: Easy thumb reach | Red: Requires stretching

✅ Natural thumb zone

The bottom third of the screen where thumbs naturally rest. This is where your most important interactions should live.

  • • Primary navigation
  • • Main action buttons
  • • Frequently used controls

⚠️ Stretch zone

Middle area that requires some thumb extension. Usable but less comfortable for frequent interactions.

  • • Secondary controls
  • • Content browsing
  • • Settings access

❌ No-go zone

Top of screen requires major hand repositioning. Reserve for passive content only.

  • • Status information
  • • Back buttons (system handles this)
  • • Non-critical indicators

These targets are large enough for confident, accurate tapping with thumbs.

Common mobile UX mistakes

These design decisions look good on desktop but make mobile apps painful to use.

Navigation at the top

Forces users to stretch or reposition their grip constantly

Tiny close buttons

Usually in top corners where they're hardest to reach accurately

Toolbar overflow

Cramming too many actions into small space

Swipe-only navigation

No visible navigation cues for discoverability

Form fields too small

Text inputs that are painful to tap and edit

Hover-dependent UI

Features that only work on desktop with mouse

Mobile usability checklist

Make these changes to dramatically improve your mobile experience.

✅ Size and spacing

• Minimum 44px touch targets

• 8px minimum spacing between tappable elements

• Larger targets for primary actions

• Test on actual devices, not just desktop

✅ Thumb-friendly layout

• Primary navigation at bottom

• Important actions in thumb zone

• Content browsing in middle area

• Status/info at top only

✅ Form optimization

• Large, easy-to-tap form fields

• Appropriate keyboard types

• Clear field labels

• Minimize required typing

✅ One-handed operation

• Assume single-thumb usage

• Provide reachability alternatives

• Minimize required precision

• Test with your non-dominant hand

The bottom line

Mobile design isn't just desktop shrunk down—it requires understanding how people actually use their devices. Design for thumbs, not cursors.

Comfortable interaction leads to happy users and better business results.
